Norman's 7 stages of Action

Biography

Donald Norman is a pioneer in the field of Human–Computer Interaction, known for his work on user-centered design, usability, and cognitive engineering. His model of interaction is influential because it aligns with our intuitive understanding of how users engage with technology.

Norman’s model of interaction is perhaps the most influential in Human–Computer Interaction, possibly because of its closeness to our intuitive understanding of the interaction between human user and computer. The user formulates a plan of action, which is then executed at the computer interface. When the plan, or part of the plan, has been executed, the user observes the computer interface to evaluate the result of the executed plan, and to determine further actions.

The interactive cycle can be divided into two major phases: execution and evaluation. These can then be subdivided into seven stages:

  1. Establishing the goal.
  2. Forming the intention.
  3. Specifying the action sequence.
  4. Executing the action.
  5. Perceiving the system state.
  6. Interpreting the system state.
  7. Evaluating the system state with respect to the goals and intentions.

The Gulfs of Execution and Evaluation

Norman uses this model to demonstrate why some interfaces cause problems to their users. He describes these in terms of the gulf of execution and the gulf of evaluation.

The gulf of execution is the difference between the user’s formulation of the actions to reach the goal and the actions allowed by the system.
The gulf of evaluation is the distance between the physical presentation of the system state and the expectation of the user.

Human Error – Slips and Mistakes

Human errors are often classified into slips and mistakes. These can be distinguished using Norman’s gulf of execution.

  • Slips: If you understand a system well, you have formulated the right action but fail to execute it correctly—for example, mistyping or accidentally pressing a button at the wrong time.
  • Mistakes: If you do not know the system well, you may not formulate the right goal—for example, thinking a magnifying glass icon is for 'finding' when it is actually for 'magnifying'.

Understanding the type of error is vital for design:

  • Slips may be corrected by better screen design, such as adding more space between buttons.
  • Mistakes require a better understanding of the system, often needing radical redesign, improved training, or a different metaphor for use.

GOMS

The GOMS model, developed by Card, Moran, and Newell, is an acronym for Goals, Operators, Methods, and Selection. It provides a framework for describing the cognitive and physical steps required to complete a task.

The Four Elements of GOMS

  • Goals: These represent what the user wants to achieve and serve as "memory points" for evaluating progress or returning to after an error.
  • Operators: These are the lowest level of analysis, representing basic actions like pressing a key or reading a dialog box.
  • Methods: These describe the different ways a goal can be achieved, such as using a menu versus a keyboard shortcut.
  • Selection: These are rules that predict which method a user will choose based on the system state or user habits.

Design Focus: GOMS saves money

A real-world application of GOMS occurred when the telephone company NYNEX used it to analyze a proposed computer system. The analysis revealed the new system would actually be slower than the old one, leading the company to abandon the project and save millions of dollars.

12.5.1 Keystroke-level model (KLM)

The Keystroke-Level Model (KLM) provides detailed predictions about user performance by focusing on the human motor system. It is designed for unit tasks—simple command sequences that typically take less than 20 seconds, such as changing a font or using search-and-replace.

KLM assumes the user is an expert and focuses solely on the execution phase of a task. The model decomposes this phase into seven specific operators:

  • K (Keystroking): Striking keys, including modifiers like Shift.
  • B (Button press): Pressing a mouse button.
  • P (Pointing): Moving the mouse to a specific target.
  • H (Homing): Moving hands between the mouse and the keyboard.
  • D (Drawing): Drawing lines using the mouse.
  • M (Mental preparation): Pausing to mentally prepare for a physical action.
  • R (System response): The time the user must wait for the system.

Operator Times

The total time to execute a task is calculated by summing the times for each operator: Texecute = TK + TB + TP + TH + TD + TM + TR.

Operator Remarks Time (s)
K Press key (Good typist 90 wpm / Non-typist) 0.12 / 1.20
B Mouse button click 0.20
P Point with mouse (Average movement) 1.10
H Home hands to/from keyboard 0.40
D Drawing – domain dependent -
M Mentally prepare 1.35

The power of KLM lies in comparison. It allows designers to compare different systems or different methods within the same system to see which is fastest without needing to conduct lengthy experiments with real users.
