Lecture 1: Introduction to HCI

What is HCI?

Human–Computer Interaction (HCI) is a field that brings together human cognitive psychology, design principles, and computer science to improve how people interact with digital systems. Its main objective is to make systems efficient, usable, and user-friendly.

Human Behavior and Learned Patterns

People often perform tasks based on habits formed early in life or by observing others. For example, when asked to draw a glass, most individuals rely on memory rather than observation. Although behaviors and habits can be modified over time, a person’s core nature tends to remain consistent.

Common Types of Interfaces

  • Graphical User Interface (GUI) – interaction through visual elements
  • Command Line Interface (CLI) – interaction using text commands
  • Voice-based Interface – interaction using spoken input
  • Gesture-based Interface – interaction through physical movements

Human Memory Systems

Human beings process and store information using different memory systems:

  • Short-Term Memory (STM): temporarily holds limited information
  • Long-Term Memory (LTM): stores information for extended periods
  • Sensory Memory: briefly retains sensory input such as sight and sound

Cognitive Neural Networks

Cognitive Neural Networks are inspired by how the human brain works. They recognize and organize information by identifying patterns like shapes, colors, and textures, similar to how humans perceive objects.

Fitts’ Law

Fitts’ Law explains how long it takes for a user to move to a target on a screen. The movement time increases when the target is far away or small in size. This principle plays an important role in interface design and influenced the development of pointing devices such as the mouse.

Chunking Technique

Chunking is a cognitive strategy that makes information easier to remember by dividing it into smaller, meaningful units. A common example is writing a phone number as 123-456-7890 instead of a long continuous sequence.

Automatic Syringe Case Study

Atomic Syringe Example

The Automatic Syringe case demonstrates how minor design flaws can result in major real-world consequences. Poor keypad layout caused users to enter incorrect values, leading to serious risks.

This case highlights that interface design is an iterative process. Initial designs are rarely perfect and must be refined through testing. Early prototypes should be simple and flexible so they can be easily changed or replaced. Techniques such as paper prototypes and quick mock-ups help designers improve interfaces efficiently.

Lecture 3: Norman’s Model and Gulf Analysis

Gulf of Execution and Gulf of Evaluation

The Gulf of Execution refers to the difficulty users face when translating their goals into actions that the system supports.

The Gulf of Evaluation refers to how hard it is for users to understand the system’s response and determine whether their goal was achieved.

Activity 5: Splitwise and Social Coordination

Applying HCI Concepts

  • Cognitive Load: Users must mentally remember individual orders and prices, placing stress on working memory.
  • Social Pressure: Talking about money in groups is uncomfortable, encouraging users to trust the app’s calculations without verification.
  • Unclear Feedback: If it is not obvious who has paid, users struggle to understand the current financial state.
  • External Cognition: By showing calculations visually, the interface reduces mental effort and improves clarity.

Design Objective

The primary goal is to minimize social tension while ensuring accurate and transparent bill splitting to prevent misunderstandings.

Gulf Analysis

Execution Gulf: The complexity involved in entering detailed orders and splits within the application.

Evaluation Gulf: The challenge users face in confirming whether the final balance matches the real payments made.
